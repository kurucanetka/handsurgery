Metacarpal Fracture


Metacarpal fractures are one of the most common fractures of the upper extremity. They are often divided into fractures of the head, neck, and shaft.

Metacarpal Head Fracture


Head fractures are rare compared to neck and shaft fractures, and you will likely not encounter these much.
Head Fractures

‚Ä¢ These are generally intraarticular. The fractures are often comminuted.

‚Ä¢ They can be seen with dorsal MP dislocations. Therefore, if you treat an MP dislocation, look out for the metacarpal head fracture.

‚Ä¢ If the patient has a metacarpal head fracture in association with a clenched fist injury (punching someone in the mouth), assume that the wound has oral contamination, and these must be treated with formal irrigation and debridement in the operating room.

‚Ä¢ Radiographic evaluation includes posteroanterior, lateral, and oblique views. A Brewerton view may be performed for better visualization of the articular surface, but this should not be necessary in an ED setting.
1 of 4
38-year-old female who was attacked by a dog. She sustained this comminuted 2nd metacarpal fracture with main articular piece completely displaced and between the metacarpals.
ED Management

‚Ä¢ Generally speaking, these fractures do not need a reduction. Please put on a splint to protect and immobilize the fracture. Have patient see a hand surgeon within 1 week.

Counseling the Patient

‚Ä¢ The treatment of metacarpal head fractures are individualized.

‚Ä¢ Nevertheless, a majority of these will require surgery. It is imperative that the patient be seen as soon as possible in a Hand Surgery clinic.
When to Call Hand Surgery

‚Ä¢ The fracture is open: the skin over the fracture is open and there is a track down to bone. Most commonly, this is due to a penetrating mechanism for metacarpal head fractures. So you will see these in knife or gun injuries.

‚Ä¢ The fracture is due to a fight bite (clenched fist) mechanism.

‚Ä¢ Otherwise, please splint to protect the fracture, then have patient follow up outpatient with a hand surgeon within 1 week.

Metacarpal Neck Fracture


Metacarpal neck fractures are common and occur most often in the ring and small fingers.
Neck Fractures

‚Ä¢ They are also referred to as "boxer's fractures," though this is a misnomer as boxers rarely suffer these injuries.

‚Ä¢ These fractures occur when a clenched fist makes contact with a solid object.

‚Ä¢ The typical deformity is apex dorsal angulation, which is appreciated on the lateral and oblique x-rays.

‚Ä¢ There are two reasons for this angulation: the volar neck is generally comminuted and the instrinsic muscles of the hand that cross the MP joint flex the metacarpal head down. Remember the intrinsic muscles flex at the MP and extend at the IPs.
Case Example 1 - Coming Soon
Emergency Department Management

‚Ä¢ A commonly taught method for closed reduction of metacarpal neck fractures is the Jahss maneuver.

‚Ä¢ This involves flexing both the MP and PIP joints to 90 degrees.. Then you push up on the proximal phalanx to exert a dorsal force on the metacarpal head. Simply put, you are just trying to put the broken piece back to where it belongs.

‚Ä¢ Despite this maneuver, even if you do it perfectly, these fractures tend to fall back into their original angulation. There is generally no need to keep torturing the patient with repeat reductions and splinting. Try it once and have them follow up within 1 week with hand surgery. (Hand surgeons may disagree here. Take advice at your own risk.)

‚Ä¢ After reduction, the hand should be immobilized in the intrinsic plus position.

‚Ä¢ The hand should never be immobilized in the Jahss position (MP and PIP joints flexed to 90 degrees) as this may result in permanent PIP stiffness and skin necrosis over the PIP joint.

‚Ä¢ If the fracture is more than a week old, obtaining a reduction will be difficult and is generally not worth attempting. These patients should be immobilized in the intrinsic-plus position and follow up with hand surgery as soon as possible.
Case Example 2 - Coming Soon
Splint

‚Ä¢ Depending on which metacarpal head is fractured, you can splint accordingly. Whichever splint you choose, splint in the intrinsic plus position.

‚Ä¢ For ring and small fingers, you can place an ulnar gutter splint.

‚Ä¢ For index and long fingers, you can place a radial gutter splint.

‚Ä¢ If you don't feel comfortable placing ulnar gutter or radial gutter splints, then you can place a volar/dorsal splint from the mid-forearm up to and including the PIP joint.

‚Ä¢ Risk of placing a volar/dorsal splint when you don't have to is that you are immobilizing joints that don't need to be immobilized. This generally won't be a problem if the patient comes to clinic within 1 week. But, if they do not, then they can have temporary or even permanent stiffness in their joints.
Counseling the Patient

‚Ä¢ The treatment of metacarpal neck fractures are individualized.

‚Ä¢ Only a minority of these will require surgery.

‚Ä¢ It is still imperative that the patient be seen within one week in a Hand Surgery clinic.

‚Ä¢ Even if the fracture heals with a moderate angulation deformity or the bone is shortened, they generally DO NOT cause functional problems.

‚Ä¢ If there is significant angulation, then we will likely recommend surgery when the patient follows up in clinic. Hand surgeons can and often disagree on which of these need surgery. The conversation on surgery should be left to the treating hand surgeon when the patient follows up.
When to Call Hand Surgery

‚Ä¢ The fracture is open. Most commonly, this is due to a penetrating mechanism (eg. knife and gun injuries). Generally speaking, punching a solid object will not cause an open fracture.
